Morning of May 22 in *Baiona, Spain*. I open the window and the room is invaded with a loud but peaceful sound of the ocean splashing into rocks. The whooshing of water hypnotizes me, a white noise louder than my thoughts. It feels like the time paused for a moment, separating what happened before from what is coming.

We don't know yet at this point how much pain we will endure over the next six days; what the satisfaction from arriving will be like; or what lessons the next 144km will teach us. We're eager to start our pilgrimage, though. From our hotel, a Spanish *Parador*, we'll step on the *camino* heading east towards *Santiago de Compostela*.

Baiona's history is vast. News of the discovery of America reached it first, but the peninsula had been strategic long before that: the Romans established themselves here around 2,000 years ago to control maritime routes. In the Middle Ages, Galician nobles expanded its fortifications, and by the 16th century, the Spanish Empire had futher reinforced its stone walls. The *Parador de Baiona*, where we slept before the walk, is itself an old fortress sitting on a peninsula protecting the bay.

I first heard about *Paradores* when reading [*Paul Graham* comparing it with other brands I respect](https://x.com/paulg/status/2033221545544319410), like Aman, Rimowa, Porsche and Lego. It felt fitting to book it, and staying at one did not disappoint. Starting in 1928, the Spanish government converted castles, palaces, monasteries and fortresses into luxurious hotels, while keeping prices more affordable (e.g less than $100 a night). 

From Baiona, I will walk with my wife and a friend. We'll make our own history through six cities: *Vigo* (27km away), *Redondela* (16km), *Pontevedra* (24km), *Calda de Reis* (26km), *Padron* (23km), and finally 28km to Santiago.

While half of the day was a pleasant coast around the bay towards Vigo, we probably took a wrong turn at some point because we walked the last 10 km beside loud traffic. My ears were buzzing with car honks, and vehicles approaching from every direction. Had we made the wrong route decision? Were the next days be like this, walking through gray outskirts, concrete roads, and the industrial edges of town?

During that second half of the day, an old pain returned in my left foot, the [same one that had haunted me in Japan some 10 months earlier](/essay/five-days-on-the-kumano-kodo). By the end, it was excruciating, an 8 out of 10.

Not a great way to start the journey.

## Day 2: Vigo to Redondela
<span class="subtitle">16km.</span>

Early morning, to escape the gray concrete from day one we decide to craft our very own route.

Vigo and Redondela touch each other, and you don't know where one ends the other starts. There's a mountain between them and the road circles around it. We open Maps.me, set our route and figure we'll just walk across it, wherever our feet may land.

The plan works out. We are rewarded with a great day. The walk alternates through greenery and tiny neighborhoods, always returning us to the trees.

I wrote that night,

> **23/May/26 | Vigo → Redondela** [REVIEW]
> <br>
> <u>Grateful</u>: for my wife, for rescuing me from the ants, and for massaging my feet after an impossible day, even though she was tired herself.
> <br>
> <u>Lesson</u>: Today I learned how a mere 10% of improved efficiency from walking sticks can be a great resource to help reduce pain, or at least delay it. Worth the purchase this morning.
> <br>
> <u>Kaizen (to improve 1%)</u>: Improve my posture with the walking sticks.
> <br>
> <u>Body</u>: My body gave a lot today. Too much. Feet pain around 6 / 10. At least not the 8 / 10 from yesterday. Calmer now.
> <br>
> <u>Mind</u>: At peace. Serenity.
> <br>
> <u>A picture</u>: A forest of ferns, almost lime green, some tips curled like a snail. In the background, the bay of Redondela. Ships.

## Day 3: Redondela to Pontevedra
<span class="subtitle">24km.</span>

> **24/May/26 | Redondela → Pontevedra** [REVIEW]
> <br>
> <u>Grateful</u>: for the smells, for being carried forward, for the sound of water in the stream, and for the sensory immersion in nature. For stepping on Pontesampaio. For the privilege to keep walking.
> <br>
> <u>Lesson</u>: to be fierce in the combat against frustrations over circumstances. To exercise my focus on the things I can control, and gratitude for the things I can't. We started late today for reasons outside my control, carrying that would have made the day worse.
> <br>
> <u>What stays behind</u>: The unintentional frustration by circumstances.
> <br>
> <u>Kaizen (to improve 1%)</u>: Stretch my calves more effortfully. Take more pauses for my feet. Walking sticks helped a lot, but were not enough. I should pause every 1h, with or without pain.
> <br>
> <u>Body</u>: Feet feels better than yesterday. I compensated a bit with my calves and they suffered the cost.
> <br>
> <u>Mind</u>: Clean.
> <br>
> <u>A picture</u>: Running water under the small red bridge. Crystalline, clean. Bare feet on the cold stone, the feeling of stars under my soles. Thoughts emptying out.

---

The focus on the countless climbs and descents, one after another, makes the perception of progress feel diffuse. The steps draw a persistent rhythm, like the hands of a clock. The sense of moving forward gets lost in the green, but the map insists on telling us that, although it feels endless, some 4 or 5km have passed and Santiago is closer. But in the grand scheme of things? Not really.

Isn't life like that too? The highs and lows take away our perspective, and we don't have a number, a distance to guide us, but it is important to always be making progress. Maybe the bad moments are just that: checkpoints.

During moments of pain, focusing on the landscape and on my breathing helped me move through the discomfort, like a meditation in motion. My left foot has hurt for 10 months, a variable pain that can reach 8 out of 10. The maintenance of pain taught me, in a visceral way, that pauses are a form of progress. There is a lot about pauses.

The tired body that said "no" would wake up in the morning saying "maybe." The pain that had screamed the day before would wake in silence. Stretching and massages at night drained the discomfort, and the mantra circling my head as I prepared my backpack to begin was, "I can."

Santiago de Compostela sits at 240m of altitude.

If the Kumano Kodo is a match, the Camino is like playing a championship. The Japanese trail is chaotic, unexpected, excruciating, and wonderful, but every day is different. The Spanish one repeats itself every day, with small variations. The terrain of the Kumano Kodo requires concentration on each step, on deciding which stone not to step on, or which space between the roots is safer for the ankle. It begins and ends, and you have achieved something important. The Camino felt like a championship where you repeat the same thing every day, with few variations.

## Day 4: Pontevedra to Caldas de Reis
<span class="subtitle">26km.</span>

> **25/May/26 | Pontevedra → Caldas de Reis** [REVIEW]
> <br>
> <u>Grateful</u>: for my foot pain improving. In general, 3 / 10, except on asphalt climbs, reaching 7 / 10. Grateful for being able to enjoy the view, the smells, and the sounds much more without that constant nagging.
> <br>
> <u>Lesson</u>: Pay closer attention to the meditative state when it arrives through silence. Give the proper focus and attention to that moment, the sensation, and experience it fully.
> <br>
> <u>What stays behind</u>: I was reflecting on which problems I can leave behind. The road invites me to drop problems along the way. With each step, each new scene, a thought can fall to the ground and stay there. In my case, when I go back home, those problems will still be there. I cannot simply leave them between one step and another. What I do know is that I will see them differently when I return, therefore what stays behind are old perspectives, the ones that no longer serve me. The new perception is peaceful, constructive, and with a conviction that I shall not waste energy with things I do not control.
> <br>
> <u>Kaizen (to improve 1%)</u>: More attention to asphalt climbs and foot pauses.
> <br>
> <u>Body</u>: Feet in pain. At night, they keep throbbing.
> <br>
> <u>Mind</u>: Clear. Tired, but lucid.
> <br>
> <u>A picture</u>: A corridor of green bamboos, knocking against each other and echoing a sharp sound, like children playing hopscotch. A thin stream of water cuts the scene, carrying a sound of peace from one ear to the other. My eyes close, thoughts disappear, and under my feet a stone bridge sustains me.

## Day 6: Padron to Santiago de Compostela
<span class="subtitle">28km.</span>

About 8km away, from a peak between the trees, we see buildings and a Ferris wheel on the horizon. From there, we descend into the valley toward Agrela, and the arrival is a new, steady climb.

> **27/May/26 | Padron → Santiago de Compostela** [REVIEW]
> <br>
> <u>Grateful</u>: for being able to carry this achievement with me. 144 km.
> <br>
> <u>Lesson</u>: the summary is about climbs, descents, and the sensation of repetition, but then the map shows that 5 km have passed. I didn't get this on the Kumano Kodo.
> <br>
> <u>What stays behind</u>: The desire or obsession to feel progress at every moment. It is necessary to have a clearly define destination, to be willing to change direction when on the wrong path, and to enjoy the journey, being present. The beauty of the green, the sound of crystalline water, and the refreshing shade make the passage of space and time much more pleasant.
> <br>
> <u>Kaizen (to improve 1%)</u>: Daily stretches and self-massage eliminate pain. During the walk, I repeated this process every night and woke up pain-free. I missed one of the days and suffered throughout the next day with discomfort. Beyond this pilgrimage, I should incorporate this process in my routine.
> <br>
> <u>Body</u>: Exhausted. The last day was rough. The foot pain was easing, but on the last day I was totally limited.
> <br>
> <u>Mind</u>: Clear. I did not feel the euphoria I was expecting when I arrived at the church. Only relief. The reward came the next day, when I finally entered the cathedral.
> <br>
> <u>A picture</u>: The architectural details of the church of Santiago. The small and infinite reliefs on the walls, carrying stories from centuries ago. Gold everywhere elevating historical figures.



2. What stayed on the road

The desire to feel progress at every moment. Progress does not always come with checkpoints or signs. Sometimes, you walk while feeling slow, but when you least expect it, you arrive at the destination. What matters is (1) having the destination clearly traced, (2) being willing to change direction when you enter the wrong road, and (3) enjoying the journey, being in the present. It is the beauty of the green, the sound of crystal-clear water, and the refreshing shade that make the passage through space and time much more bearable.
