Morning of May 22 in *Baiona, Spain*. I open the window and the room is invaded with a loud but peaceful sound of ocean splashing into rocks. The whooshing of water hypnotizes me, a white noise louder than my thoughts. It feels like the time paused for a moment, separating what happened before from what is coming.

I don't know yet at this point how much pain we will endure over the next six days; what the satisfaction from arriving will be like; or what lessons the next 144km will teach me. Whatever's ahead, I can't wait to start.

## Walk

When I walked the [*Kumano Kodo*](/essay/five-days-on-the-kumano-kodo) in Japan, my objective was stepping back, metaphorically speaking, and reconnecting the primitive self with nature. Search for some meaning. You know, what one does when they're on the verge of turning *fourty*.

My objective for these six days in the Camino was different. I wasn't looking for anything other than to disconnect and work on my mental health with [meditation](/essay/meditation). A walk is perfect for that because it serves as a gravitational force that pulls thoughts into a steady and slower pace matching the scenary passing by. Each step becomes a beat in the music of the mind.

The rules remain the same as my *Kumano Kodo* walk: no social media, music, podcast or anything robbing me from the present.

With me are my wife and a friend, *Marcos*, on a walk that will traverse *Vigo* (27km away), *Redondela* (16km), *Pontevedra* (24km), *Calda de Reis* (26km), *Padron* (23km), and finally 28km to Santiago de Compostela.

## Day 1: Baiona to Vigo
<span class="subtitle">27km</span>

Our pilgrimage starts around a calm bay dotted with boats, floating in history. From our hotel, a Spanish *Parador*, we step on the *camino* heading east towards *Santiago de Compostela*. 

Our requirements for the walk included fitting in 6 days, being more than 100km, including the ocean, and ideally start south so we could connect from *Lisbon*. *Porto* didn't fit the timeline; *Tui* didn't expose the Atlantic enough; *Oia* required special logistics; starting from *Vigo* wouldn't expose us to a full day of water extending to the horizon. Baiona felt like the perfect choice at 127km from Santiago.

Baiona's history was yet another reason. News of the discovery of America reached it first, but the peninsula had been strategic long before that: the Romans established themselves here around 2,000 years ago to control maritime routes. In the Middle Ages, Galician nobles expanded its fortifications, and by the 16th century, the Spanish Empire had futher reinforced its stone walls. The *Parador de Baiona*, where we slept before the walk, is itself an old fortress sitting on a peninsula protecting the bay.

I first heard about *Paradores* when reading [*Paul Graham*'s comparison to other brands I respect](https://x.com/paulg/status/2033221545544319410), such as Aman, Rimowa, Porsche and Lego. Starting in 1928, the Spanish government converted castles, palaces, monasteries and fortresses into luxurious hotels, while keeping prices more affordable. It felt fitting to book it, and it did not disappoint.

The walk by the coast was perfect. Despite the high temperature, the wind was gentle and refreshing. Ships could be seen where the blue water meets the sky, faint on the horizon. Between the ocean and the sidewalk, bands of sand stretch far enough to look like a carpet. People walk barefoot while their dogs hurry to sniff around any salted corner they can find. For about 15km, we moved along through *Playa Ladeira*, *Playa América*, *Playa Patos*, *playa*, *playa*, *playa*. The breeze on my face gave me unreasonable optimism.

What if the next five days are as perfect as this?

When we turned inland toward *Vigo* for the remaining 10 kms, my ears started buzzing with relentless car honks and my heel started pulsating with pain. Suddenly, we were being swallowed by a jungle of concrete. It was almost funny to end the day with an internal competition between thoughts and the loud traffic. It became an opportunity to practice those meditation skills, while grimacing in response to the pain.

My left heel ached like I had stepped on a nail. It wasn't an unknown pain because it had haunted me in Japan 10 months earlier. In some ways, I expected it, but I didn't pay much attention because it had faded away. However, a couple weeks before this walk, it started pulsating again, with intensity of 1 out of 10. I rushed to a doctor to investigate, but the tests came back inconclusive. It was too late and I would never cancel a walk because of a intensity-one pain, right?

What I did not expect was that it would return with such intensity. By the time I arrived at the hotel, it was excruciating, *An 8 out of 10*.

## Day 2: Vigo to Redondela
<span class="subtitle">16km.</span>

Early morning, to escape the gray concrete from day one we decide to craft our very own route.

Vigo and Redondela touch each other, and you don't know where one ends the other starts. There's a mountain between them and the road circles around it. We open Maps.me, set our route and figure we'll just walk across it, wherever our feet may land.

The plan works out. We are rewarded with a great day. The walk alternates through greenery and tiny neighborhoods, always returning us to the trees.

The focus on the countless climbs and descents, one after another, makes the perception of progress feel diffuse. The steps draw a persistent rhythm, like the hands of a clock. The sense of moving forward gets lost in the green, but the map insists on telling us that, although it feels endless, some 4 or 5km have passed and Santiago is closer. But in the grand scheme of things? Not really.

Isn't life like that too? The highs and lows take away our perspective, and we don't have a number, a distance to guide us, but it is important to always be making progress. Maybe the bad moments are just that: checkpoints.

During moments of pain, focusing on the landscape and on my breathing helped me move through the discomfort, like a meditation in motion. My left foot has hurt for 10 months, a variable pain that can reach 8 out of 10. The maintenance of pain taught me, in a visceral way, that pauses are a form of progress. There is a lot about pauses.

The tired body that said "no" would wake up in the morning saying "maybe." The pain that had screamed the day before would wake in silence. Stretching and massages at night drained the discomfort, and the mantra circling my head as I prepared my backpack to begin was, "I can."

Santiago de Compostela sits at 240m of altitude.

If the Kumano Kodo is a match, the Camino is like playing a championship. The Japanese trail is chaotic, unexpected, excruciating, and wonderful, but every day is different. The Spanish one repeats itself every day, with small variations. The terrain of the Kumano Kodo requires concentration on each step, on deciding which stone not to step on, or which space between the roots is safer for the ankle. It begins and ends, and you have achieved something important. The Camino felt like a championship where you repeat the same thing every day, with few variations.

My mind isn’t exactly sleepy. It feels contracted, like it has lost cognitive range of motion after holding tension for too long.

[mental stiffness]
[reduced cognitive range of motion: a tired mind can still move, but not freely. It avoids ambiguity, resists context-switching, and struggles to explore.]

[REVIEW, meditation] The mind is this wild horse running and jumping around. Meditation is the rope tossed around its neck. Whenever the horse bolts, you give the rope a gentle pull. Meditating over long periods calms the mind, slowing it down to the pace of the walk.

[REVIEW] The difference between good and bad days is where our attention goes, so paying attention to what we pay attention to is everything. Gaining control over our perception is a worthwhile exercise for a good life, but it doesn't come by default, it's a choice. Over time, I feel the endless sensory stimulation of modern life puts my mind adrift, and I find myself focusing on things that are beyond my control. A walk blocks that stimuli, and for long enough, unlocks a level of self-control and intentionality that a don't feel the average vacation does.

> <u>Body</u>: Exhausted. The last day was rough. The foot pain was easing, but on the last day I was totally limited.
> <br>
> <u>Mind</u>: Clear. I did not feel the euphoria I was expecting when I arrived at the church. Only relief. The reward came the next day, when I finally entered the cathedral.
> <br>
> <u>A picture</u>: The architectural details of the church of Santiago. The small and infinite reliefs on the walls, carrying stories from centuries ago. Gold everywhere elevating historical figures.



2. What stayed on the road

The desire to feel progress at every moment. Progress does not always come with checkpoints or signs. Sometimes, you walk while feeling slow, but when you least expect it, you arrive at the destination. What matters is (1) having the destination clearly traced, (2) being willing to change direction when you enter the wrong road, and (3) enjoying the journey, being in the present. It is the beauty of the green, the sound of crystal-clear water, and the refreshing shade that make the passage through space and time much more bearable.
